Simple Counting.
The reason we learn binary is because everyone is already using it. It's not so hard as you think.


Let's take a look at the number 268. It has three places and no decimal. If we say that 1 indicates the presence of a digit at the place, then the sequence 111 would indicate a number has three digits. Under a Simple Counting model, all you'd have to do is repeat the pattern a maximum of 8 times (8 is the highest digit), for the following sequence:

001001011011011011111111268

Simple, right? Yet also very tedious. What we are looking for is a more compact way of expressing numbers. One way of doing this is multiplicative. What we have here is battery, and that battery will store avalue for you, but you have to charge it up, starting from right to left.
The first bit on the right should be 1. If it isn't 1 then you have 0. Simple, right? Let's skip on to 2.
The second bit on the right stores 2^1, which is just 2. So what can we do? Let's see what happens when we put 1 in the two-place. 112+1=3 Oh no. 2 + 1 is not 2, but 3. But, we know where the extra 1 is. It's in the 1 place. We just delete that. Now our 2 bit number looks like this:
10 =2

2 plus 0 equals 2. We are now smart enough to count to 2. Yay. And if we put the 1 back, we can also count to 3.
Counting to 4: Since 4>3, we need to add another bit. The third bit has the value of 2^2 or 2*2, which if you might be wondering equals 4. We're already done. Just put 1 in the 3-place.
1002^2=4

Since we know how to count to 3, we can just add that to make any number up to 7.

Quaternary

Quaternary is an extremely useful number system to learn for the sole reason that the nucleotide sequences in DNA are in base-four. Incidentally, base-four is the third closest whole base to e that isn't one, and one is kind of silly. You might be noticing this convergence is less than coincidence.
